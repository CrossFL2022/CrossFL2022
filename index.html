Workshop on "Breaking Community Barriers in Federated Learning - Algorithms, Systems and Co-designs"

Federated Learning (FL) has recently emerged as the de facto framework for distributed machine learning (ML) beyond data centers that preserves the privacy of data, especially in the proliferation of mobile and edge devices with their increasing capacity for sensing, computation and storage. To fully utilize the vast amount of distributed, diverse and privately owned data that is stored across these devices, FL provides a platform on which local devices can build their own local models whose training processes can be synchronized via sharing model updates rather than raw data. Such potential use of FL has since then led to an explosive attention from the ML, systems, optimization, signal processing and wireless networking communities. During recent years, a growing amount of both theoretical and empirical research pushes FL closer to being the new standard of ML as a democratized data analytic service.

FL-related research is evolving at a rapid pace and is penetrating into almost every scientific and technologic society. However, as FL comes closer to being deployable in real-world scenarios, many open problems in FL today cannot be solved solely by researchers in one community. For example, most ML algorithms were originally designed for data center environments, but learning in data centers is markedly distinct from learning on devices because devices are resource constrained (e.g., bandwidth, memory, and energy), and they can be less reliable due to factors such as privacy leakage and malicious attacks. Designing most effcient and reliable FL algorithms require leveraging expertises from systems, security, signal processing and networking communities. On the other hand, current computing and networking systems are designed for content-centric applications using metrics such as latency and throughput, but FL traffc is fundamentally different from its traditional counterpart. Specifically, most FL algorithms are iterative, but not every iteration of algorithms or every bit of data is equally important. Therefore, designing most effcient and scalable computing and networking systems require leveraging collaborative advances from ML, data mining, and optimization communities.

In light of the differences in education backgrounds, cultures, toolboxes, viewpoints, and design principles of different communities, this workshop aims to break community barriers and bring researchers from pertinent communities together to address open problems in FL. More importantly, this workshop aims to stimulate discussion among experts in different fields (e.g., industry and academia) and identify new problems that remain underexplored from an interdisciplinary perspective. Specifically, the topics of interest include but not limited to:

  • Adversarial robustness and security in Federated Learning
  • Data privacy in Federated Learning
  • Mathematical optimization for Federated Learning
  • Communication and networking for Federated Learning
  • Hardware acceleration for Federated Learning
  • Hardware and algorithms co-designs for Federated Learning
  • Algorithms and systems co-designs for Federated Learning

We hope that this workshop will serve as a forum for researchers across different disciplines to bring forward and discuss challenging topics, share new ideas and exchange experience in the deployment of such systems, both from a theoretical and experimental perspective.
